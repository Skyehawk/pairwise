Introduction

This help page covers a variety of topics get the most from your collected data for use with the Pairwise tool to perform singular value decomposition (SVD) rank recovery (i.e., generate picklists from comparing teams against each other). There are some limitations and strategies the end user can employ to ensure a smooth user experience, limit potential issues, and ultimately achieve a greater success rate.

The Pairwise tool is a season-independent scouting analysis aid, capable of performing standalone rank recovery generation for all manner of tasks. The purpose of this tool is to provide scouting support for:

  • Teams that may not have enough members to scout efficiently
  • Teams that may not have time to create their own data collection methods
  • Teams that rely on community statistical tools
  • Teams that would like a starting point based on team preference to begin scouting meetings
  • Developing team comparison/analytical skills to expedite scouting meetings
  • Scouting on vibes ðŸ¤™
Pairwise is not suited for / capable of:
  • Any form of data storage - there is a rudimentary calculator for .csv contents provided by the webapp for convienence but it is not capable of any data storage. This may change in the future, but for now you will need to manage your own output. A guide on how to do this will be provided.
  • Analyizing existing quantitative data, if your data is already in this form you likely are better off sorting it by that metric to begin with
  • Fixing pooly collected/ inconsistent data - garbage in, garbage out
The underpinning philosophy on development is this should be a tool that is instantaneously usable for data processing with no special alterations from season-to-season for game-specific tasks. Updates to Pairwise are not needed each year on kickoff, and development in the weeks leading up to during the competition season will be extremely limited and focused on bugs, not adding new features.

Usage is simple, relying on the pairwise comparison of items (hereafter teams) in order to reconstruct a ranking. The more observations collected, the greater the separability of teams becomes. Consistency is key in these observations; practice and a rubric may make this easier. A rudimentary calculator for the purposes of limited data generation is provided (you will need to place the calculated values in your data .csv yourself). Pairwise rank rconstruction has been tested with more than 500 individual observations. More on this in Getting Started.

Fundamentally, the process of ranking should encourage discussion in the stands among scouts, building team analysis skills. A picklist from these observations, generated by Pairwise, should provide a useful starting point, whereafter discussion and/or additional data can be used to adjust the Pairwise generated picklist for a team's specific needs.

The core functionality of the pairwise tool is provided here as a community benefit free of charge as well as in a GitHub repo and a Google Colab notebook for those who wish to have offline capabilities and contingency plans in place in the event of outages.

Getting Started

Since Pairwise focuses on subjective rankings of aspects that may be difficult or time-consuming to quantify (such as driver skill, defense/avoiding prowess, robot speed, or "vibes"), it may be useful to generate a rubric on what constitutes a good team in this category. I recommend you start simple, by generating some guidelines on which attributes make a team attractive and which detract for your chosen use case. I would also not try to evaluate more than 2 different qualitative attributes per match; this will simply be too much to handle at the start.

A list of attributes for robot speed may look like:

Pros:

  • Fastest driving robot on alliance
  • Always the most lined up with gamepiece/field element on the first attempt
  • Touches a game piece - Owns it
  • Goes around blockages more than alliance partners that may slow them down vs through
  • Spends the least amount of time stopped at a feeder station, the robot that is always in motion
Cons:
  • Likes to run into and push other robots (including alliance partners)
  • Often clips or runs into major elements of the field more than alliance partners
  • Spends the most time chasing game pieces around the field
  • The robot on the alliance that may move game pieces around without purpose
You may have noticed that we are defining everything relative to the alliance partners, not to the best team at the event. This allows us to evaluate just what is happening in front of us, hopefully reducing the effects of recency bias or confirmation bias. You could have 3 robots that are absolutely horrible in that match (maybe one has electrical issues, one has a broken mechanism, and one was designed poorly). Your goal is to rank the best of what is in front of you right now, just in this particular match.

There is nothing stopping you from including observations of when a team goes head-to-head with another team on the other alliance; you could also include observations in the .csv from back-to-back matches. Just be aware of the aforementioned biases. With the above as a rough rubric, you can use it to rank the best and worst teams on an alliance. You can generate the pairwise comparisons youself (see the Data Collection help page) or use the Data Input & Formatter Tool.
